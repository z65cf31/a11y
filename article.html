The social model of disability

The social model of disability is a way of thinking which states that disability is caused by barriers within society rather than an individual’s impairment. Through this lens, it’s possible to conceive that if all barriers could be removed, disability would cease to exist.

Team Accessibility (Team A11Y ) helps squads to remove the digital barriers and make ING websites and apps accessible to all people, with or without disabilities. The help consists of co-creation, laying out the digital baseline and provide training for accessibility champions.

Why we promote accessibility

Our believe is that digital equality is a human right for websites, mobile applications, and digital content. But it doesn’t stop there. We need to do it for quality, innovation, compliance, relations, user experience and much much more. An Alphabet of Accessibility Issues

01. Content provides a logical and hierarchical heading structure

A good heading structure enables people to scan a page/screen quickly and understand the structure of the content. Screen reader users can also use headings to quickly navigate within a page or screen.

Other things to consider, in order to make headings more usable for screen reader users:

  • Lists and headings used together to mark up content should be avoided
  • For a correct heading outline it is sometimes necessary to hide them visually
  • Headings should be coded around content that doesn’t change, i.e. a category name such as ‘Banking News’ rather than a news item that will update frequently
  • Headings should not overwhelm a page as this can undermine their usefulness for screen reader users when navigating, a maximum of 7-9 headings should suffice

03. Links are Links and Buttons are Buttons

How to choose between a link and a button:

  1. Will this control be used to initiate an immediate action? It’s a button!
  2. Is the action to navigate to another page? It’s a link!

General guidelines on making the choice:

  • Use command buttons for:
    • Primary commands
    • Displaying windows used to gather input or making choices, even if they are secondary commands
    • Destructive or irreversible actions, as well as commitment within wizards and page flows
  • Use links for navigation to another page, window, or Help topic; display definitions; and command menus

04. Max width of links, lists, input fields and paragraphs

Links, lists, input fields and paragraphs will have a max width of 600 CSS pixels to keep everything readable. Selects, text fields, radio buttons, checkboxes etc are considered input fields. Note that the max label width is often a bit smaller, as lots of components have elements that take in space as well, for instance, the actually checkbox in a checkbox component.

For those who are into the subject of readability, we know this is in the upper regions of an ideally desired max length. So this means that in a card of let’s say 800px the width of a paragraph or input field label will never be larger than 600px. So yes, that means white space at some point.

Note! The max label width is often a bit smaller, as lots of components have elements that take in space as well, for instance, the actually checkbox in a checkbox component.

05. Accessibility and DoD are spelt out in full

Accessibility and Dod are spelt out in full the first time on the page and acronyms in general are avoided as much as possible. The objective of this technique is to provide expansions or definitions for abbreviations by using the abbr element.

Note! It is always appropriate to use the <abbr> element for any abbreviation, including acronyms and initialisms. When using HTML 4 and XHTML, initialisms and acronyms may be marked up using the acronym element. In HTML5 and newer versions of HTML the acronym element was marked as obsolete in favor of the more general <abbr> element.

07. Emphasized text marked up as such using structure to convey semantic meaning

The text "digital equality is a human right" is emphasized using the <strong> element. The <figcaption> elements is emphasized using the <em> element.

Note! Semantic mark-up such as <strong>, <em>, etc. should be used over purely visual formatting using CSS to indicate meaningful aspects such as text formatting. Additionally, structures such as lists, and headings should be used to indicate relationships of items.

As the <b> (bold) and <i> (italic) elements are both only styling elements we should avoid using those as they don't convey programmatic meaning / additional importance.

08. Links with distinctive appearance and communicating function

Links have a distinctive appearance using icons, the functional icon name and the non-color cues: underline and "open in new window" icon. The functional icon has a WAI-ARIA role of image and descriptive accessible name.

Note! Opening pages in a new tab / window is higly discouraged whenever possible. The best practice for all links is to stay in the same tab / window as lots of people don't know what browser tabs are, have no idea pages open in new tabs and can't figure out why the back button doesn't work. This is even more often experienced on mobile.

Opening new windows automatically when a link is activated can be disorienting for people who have difficulty perceiving visual content, and for some people with cognitive disabilities, if they are not warned in advance.

When the choice is to open links in a new window all users must be informed of this function. Also users who use screen readers / assistive technology.

12. Link text descriptive on its own

The use of the link text contains a brief description instead of "click here..." or "read more..." to make the link text more descriptive and to convey the purpose of the link. This is especially important for people using link lists and who don't read all of the content on all pages. Also indexing these links for SEO, like for Google, does provide information on the purpose of the links.

Note! As links (the "Hyper Text" in HTML) are always supposed to go to a new page, the best link text is the text of the title of the page it goes to. So the link to the "Contact" page should be "Contact" and to the "Request Account" page a "Request Account" link is the most appropriate.

Text closely related to the page title of where the link goes to is also allowed. So a "want to contact us?" link going to the "contact" page is also fine.

13. Only the first letter of words capitalized

Starting with capital at the beginning of a word may be used but sentences or phrases IN ALL CAPITALS is not a good idea. Capitalized content is more difficult to read and may give the impression that the author is shouting. Using an exclamation point or use of <em> or <strong> is often way better.

Note! Firstly, some screen readers read capitalized text letter-by-letter. And this occasionally even occurs when the HTML is in sentence-case and the CSS forces the capitalization.

The second problem is that capital letters are harder to read (for everyone, but especially people with reading disabilities).

14. Centrally aligned text is avoided

Centered and left aligned text are the most widely used text alignments. How you use these text alignments can either help or harm your users when they read.

Left aligned

  • Left aligned text is easier to read
  • The text and thus the page is less noisy
  • People who zoom the page have a straight left edge
  • With horizontal scrolling the beginning of previous / next sentences are in view
  • Users can read each line by simply moving their eyes to the left edge each time
  • Paragraphs reading is faster because eyes don’t have to work as hard

Center aligned

  • When centering text the starting place of each line changes
  • Users have to work harder to find where each line begins to continue reading
  • No consistent place for moving the eyes to when completing each line
  • The text and thus the page is generally more noisy

Note! When chosing center aligned text one mistake you should never make is to combine centered text with left aligned text. This is because the unsymmetrical line lengths of the paragraph can give the headline the appearance that it’s slightly off-center. You also lose the symmetrical appearance of your paragraph. Avoid mixing the two together so that your text doesn’t look unbalanced.

The direction property specifies the text direction/writing direction which may change according to the language.

HTML Example


body {
  direction: ltr;
  text-align: left;
}

WCAG Success Criteria

  • 1.4.8 Visual Presentation Level AAA

15. Full justification for text is avoided

Studies have consistently shown that left-aligned text is the easiest to read contrary to justified text. How you use these text alignments can either help or harm your users when they read.

Left aligned

  • Left aligned text is easier to read
  • A ragged right margin (left-justified text) makes it easier to find the next line of text
  • Paragraphs reading is faster because eyes don’t have to work as hard

Justified text

  • Affects the readability by overflow of letters
  • Present problems for people with Dyslexia
  • Creates what’s been termed "rivers of white", referring to the spaces of white
  • Increase the need for scrolling for people who rely on zooming
  • For people with specific eye conditions or cognitive or learning difficulties the effect of kerning is that it creates uneven white spaces which make lines of print hard to follow

HTML Example


body {
  direction: ltr;
  text-align: left;
}

WCAG Success Criteria

  • 1.4.8 Visual Presentation Level AAA

    Third Requirement: Techniques to ensure text is not justified (aligned to both the left and the right margins)

    1. C19: Specifying alignment either to the left OR right in CSS (CSS)OR
    2. G172: Providing a mechanism to remove full justification of text OR
    3. G169: Aligning text on only one side
  • Guideline 3.1 – Readable
    • Avoiding text that is fully justified (to both left and right margins) in a way that causes poor spacing between words or characters
    • Using left-justified text for languages that are written left to right and right-justified text for languages that are written right-to-left
